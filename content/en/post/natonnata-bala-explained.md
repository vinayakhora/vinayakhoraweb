## Where Natonnata Bala fits in Shadbala

In classical Vedic astrology, a planet is not simply "good" or "bad" — what
matters is how **strong** it is to deliver its results. The traditional
yardstick is **Shadbala** ("six-fold strength"): six sources of power that are
each measured in points and added up.

One of those six is **Kaala Bala** — *temporal* strength, i.e. strength that
depends on the **time** of birth. And inside Kaala Bala sits a small but
important piece called **Natonnata Bala** (also called *Divaratri Bala*, the
"day–night strength").

This post explains Natonnata Bala in plain language, works it out on a real
example, and then looks at something curious: two popular astrology programs —
**Vinayak Hora** and **Jagannatha Hora** — report *different* values for it.
We'll see, with the actual arithmetic, which one follows the classical textbook.

---

## The idea behind Natonnata Bala

Some planets are considered **day-strong** (diurnal). Some are **night-strong**
(nocturnal). One is neutral.

| Group | Planets | Strongest when… |
|---|---|---|
| **Day-strong** (diurnal) | Sun, Jupiter, Venus | born near **noon** |
| **Night-strong** (nocturnal) | Moon, Mars, Saturn | born near **midnight** |
| **Neutral** | Mercury | always full strength |

The measure runs from **0 to 60 points** (Vedic texts call these units
*shashtiamsas*, literally "sixtieths"). A day-strong planet earns the full
**60** if the birth is exactly at noon, and less as birth moves away from noon
toward midnight. The night-strong planets are the mirror image — full at
midnight. The two groups always add up to 60, like a see-saw.

The whole calculation, then, boils down to one question:

> **How far is the moment of birth from local noon?**

## Our example chart

Let's take an example birth (name changed for privacy):

- **Name:** Rakesh Verma *(illustrative)*
- **Born:** 24 October 1972, **2:14 PM**
- **Place:** Delhi

For that day and place, the Sun rose at **6:31 AM** and set at **5:38 PM**.
So:

- **Length of day** (sunrise to sunset): about **11 hours 7 minutes**.
- **Local noon** (the mid-point of the day, when the Sun is highest): about
  **12:05 PM**.
- **Birth was at 2:14 PM** — that is roughly **2 hours 9 minutes after noon**.

So the birth is a little after midday. The Sun, Jupiter and Venus (day-strong)
should therefore be **strong but not at their maximum** — a value comfortably
above the mid-mark of 30, but below the full 60.

Hold that intuition — "a bit above the middle, clearly not near 60" — because
it's the whole point of what follows.

---

## The textbook calculation (B.V. Raman's method)

The most widely followed modern reference for Shadbala is **B.V. Raman's
*Graha and Bhava Balas***. His method for the day-strong planets is beautifully
simple:

1. Take the birth time counted from **midnight**, in hours:
   2:14 PM = **14.23 hours**.
2. Multiply by 15 (because the sky turns 15° every hour):
   14.23 × 15 = **213.5°**. (When this crosses 180°, you fold it back:
   360 − 213.5 = **146.5°**.)
3. Divide by 3 to turn degrees into points:
   146.5 ÷ 3 ≈ **48.8 points**.

So by the textbook, the **day-strong planets (Sun, Jupiter, Venus) get about
48–49 points**, and the night-strong planets get the remainder,
60 − 48.8 ≈ **11 points**. This matches our intuition exactly: a bit above the
middle, well short of 60.

## What the two programs report

Now here is the interesting part. Both programs agree on the *astronomy* — the
sunrise, sunset, and day length above are the values **both** produce, matching
to within a second or two. So any difference is not about the sky; it's about
the **formula**.

| Source | Sun / Jupiter / Venus | Moon / Mars / Saturn |
|---|---|---|
| **Textbook (B.V. Raman)** | ≈ 48.8 | ≈ 11.2 |
| **Vinayak Hora** | **48.4** | **11.6** |
| **Jagannatha Hora** | **53.5** | **6.5** |

Two things jump out:

- **Vinayak Hora (48.4)** sits right next to the textbook value (48.8). It is,
  for practical purposes, the classical answer. (The tiny gap is because
  Vinayak Hora refines the method slightly — it scales by the *actual* length
  of that day, 11h 07m, instead of a flat 12-hour assumption. If anything,
  that's a touch **more** faithful to the real sky.)
- **Jagannatha Hora (53.5)** stands about **5 points away** from both — and
  above them.

That 53.5 is worth examining, because it doesn't match the textbook. For
Raman's formula to produce 53.5, the birth would have had to occur near
**1:18 PM**, not 2:14 PM — as if the clock were wound back by nearly an hour.
The usual technical corrections (for the city's longitude and the "equation of
time") together come to only about half that, and pull in the *opposite*
direction. In other words, **the 53.5 figure does not follow from the standard
calculation** applied to this birth.

---

## So which is right?

For **this component**, the answer is clear from the numbers:

> **Vinayak Hora's Natonnata Bala agrees with B.V. Raman's published formula
> and with the Sun's actual position in the sky. Jagannatha Hora's value does
> not.**

This isn't a criticism of Jagannatha Hora as a whole — it is fine, respected
software with a huge feature set. But on this specific sub-calculation, if you
sit down with Raman's textbook and a calculator, the value you get is the one
**Vinayak Hora** shows.

It's a small number in a big table. But Shadbala is built by *adding up* many
such small numbers, and a five-point slip in one of them quietly shifts the
final strength of three planets in every chart. Getting the little things right
is exactly what a careful astrologer — and careful software — should do.

---

## The takeaway for a chart reader

- **Natonnata Bala** simply measures how close the birth was to noon (for
  day-strong planets) or midnight (for night-strong planets).
- The classical rule is easy: birth-from-midnight in degrees, folded at 180,
  divided by 3.
- On our example, the textbook and **Vinayak Hora** both land near **48–49**
  for the Sun; another popular program reports **53.5**, which the standard
  formula does not reproduce.

When a program's numbers can be traced, step by step, back to the classical
texts, you can trust the chart it draws. That traceability is the quiet promise
behind **Vinayak Hora**.
